

# Problem statement

Let $\operatorname{lcm}(1,2,\dots,n)$ denote the least common multiple of $1,2,\dots,n$.
Is it true that, for all $k \ge 1$,
\[
\operatorname{lcm}\bigl(1,2,\dots,p_{k+1}-1\bigr) \;<\; p_k\,\operatorname{lcm}\bigl(1,2,\dots,p_k\bigr)?
\]

# Proposed approaches to the Problem by the problem solver

## Problem_restatement

Let \(p_k\) be the \(k\)-th prime and \(L(n)=\operatorname{lcm}(1,2,\dots,n)\). The question asks whether for every \(k\ge 1\),
\[
L(p_{k+1}-1)\;<\; p_k\,L(p_k).
\]
Equivalently, whether the “extra growth” of the lcm from \(p_k\) up to just before the next prime is always less than a multiplicative factor \(p_k\).

## Assumptions or clarifications

None.

## Approaches

### **Approach 1 (Index 1): Prime-power “jump factor” reformulation + prime-gap control (conditional route)**

**Name:** Reduce to prime powers inside the prime gap; control them via prime-gap bounds (e.g. Cramér/Legendre-type).

**High-level idea:**

A key observation is that \(L(n)\) only changes when you pass a prime power threshold. Between consecutive primes \(p_k < p_{k+1}\), there are no primes, so \(L(p_{k+1}-1)\) differs from \(L(p_k)\) only because some *prime powers* \(q^a\) (with \(a\ge 2\)) might lie in \((p_k,\,p_{k+1}-1]\).

Moreover, by Bertrand’s postulate \(p_{k+1}<2p_k\), so for any fixed prime \(q\), you cannot cross *two* successive powers \(q^a\) and \(q^{a+1}\) in that short multiplicative window; hence each contributing prime \(q\) appears at most once. This makes the ratio
\[
\frac{L(p_{k+1}-1)}{L(p_k)}
\]
a **squarefree product** of certain primes \(q\), namely those for which the next prime power \(q^{a(q)}\) falls into the prime gap.

Then the inequality becomes a clean statement:
\[
\prod_{\substack{q\ \text{prime}\\ \exists a\ge 2:\ p_k<q^a<p_{k+1}}} q \;<\; p_k.
\]
The “dangerous” case is when there are **several prime squares** \(q^2\) in the gap, because each such \(q\) is \(\gtrsim \sqrt{p_k}\), and two such factors already force the product \(\ge p_k\). This is exactly the obstruction Erdős–Graham mention. 

So one natural strategy is: prove strong enough upper bounds on prime gaps so that (eventually) there is at most one prime square between consecutive primes, and similarly control higher powers and small primes.

**Detailed plan:**

1. **Write \(L(n)\) in prime-power form**:
   \[
   L(n)=\prod_{q\le n} q^{\lfloor \log_q n\rfloor},
   \]
   or equivalently \(\log L(n)=\psi(n)\) (Chebyshev’s \(\psi\)-function). 

2. **Compare exponents at \(n=p_k\) and \(n=p_{k+1}-1\)**:
   for each prime \(q\le p_k\), the exponent increases by
   \[
   \lfloor \log_q (p_{k+1}-1)\rfloor - \lfloor \log_q(p_k)\rfloor.
   \]

3. **Use Bertrand’s postulate** (\(p_{k+1}<2p_k\)) to show:
   - for any \(q\ge 2\), \((p_k,p_{k+1}-1]\subset(p_k,2p_k)\) cannot contain two consecutive powers \(q^a, q^{a+1}\),
   - hence the exponent jump for each \(q\) is either \(0\) or \(1\),
   - thus
     \[
     \frac{L(p_{k+1}-1)}{L(p_k)}=\prod_{q\in S_k} q
     \]
     for a certain set \(S_k\) of primes.

4. **Reformulate the target inequality** as:
   \[
   \prod_{q\in S_k} q < p_k.
   \]

5. **Split \(S_k\) by the relevant exponent \(a\):**
   define
   \[
   S_k(a)=\{q:\ q^a\in(p_k,p_{k+1})\},
   \]
   so \(S_k=\bigcup_{a\ge 2} S_k(a)\) (disjoint in \(q\), since only one exponent jump can occur per prime \(q\)).

6. **Control squares \(a=2\)**:
   \(q^2\in(p_k,p_{k+1})\) means \(q\in(\sqrt{p_k},\sqrt{p_{k+1}})\).
   If you can show \(\sqrt{p_{k+1}}-\sqrt{p_k}<1\), then there is at most one integer in that interval, hence at most one prime \(q\), hence at most one prime square in the gap. This is a prime-gap condition \(p_{k+1}-p_k < \sqrt{p_k}+\sqrt{p_{k+1}}\sim 2\sqrt{p_k}\), i.e. “Legendre-flavored”.

7. **Control higher powers \(a\ge 3\)** similarly:
   \(q^a\in(p_k,p_{k+1})\) means \(q\in(p_k^{1/a},p_{k+1}^{1/a})\). These root-intervals are much shorter than the square-root one.

8. **Handle “small primes trouble”:**
   for small \(q\) (2,3,5,…) the next power can be relatively close; you’d need a careful case split (e.g. show only finitely many \(k\) allow simultaneously \(2^m,3^n,5^r,\dots\) in one prime gap in a way that makes \(\prod q\ge p_k\)).

9. **Conclude under a strong prime-gap hypothesis**:
   if you assume a conjecture like Cramér’s bound \(p_{k+1}-p_k = O((\log p_k)^2)\), then \(\sqrt{p_{k+1}}-\sqrt{p_k}=O((\log p_k)^2/\sqrt{p_k})\to 0\), so eventually there are no prime squares in gaps at all, and higher powers are even easier; one would then finish by checking finitely many small \(k\) computationally.

**Required tools or theorems:**

- Prime-power formula for \(\operatorname{lcm}(1,\dots,n)\), Chebyshev \(\psi\) function identity \(\log L(n)=\psi(n)\).   
- Bertrand’s postulate \(p_{k+1}<2p_k\).  
- Prime-gap conjectures or strong bounds (Legendre-type, Cramér-type) if aiming for a full proof.  
- Careful elementary handling of small bases (2,3,5,…) where many powers exist.

**Main obstacles:**

- The core obstacle is ruling out “too many” primes \(q\) with \(p_k<q^2<p_{k+1}\); Erdős–Graham explicitly highlight this as a major barrier and link it to Legendre-type difficulty.   
- Existing unconditional prime-gap bounds are far too weak to force \(\sqrt{p_{k+1}}-\sqrt{p_k}<1\) uniformly.
- Even if squares are controlled, one must still deal with the possibility that many *different* small primes have a power crossing inside the gap.

**Expected difficulty:** Very High (unconditional); Medium–High (conditional on strong gap conjectures).

**Estimated viability score:** 85/100 (as a framework that gives conditional results); 15/100 (as an unconditional plan with current technology).

**Notes on similarity to other approaches:** Distinct (this is the clean “structural reduction” approach; others focus on analytic bounds or counterexample search).

### **Approach 2 (Index 2): Analytic-number-theory bounds via \(\psi\) and counting prime powers in prime-free intervals (partial results / density results)**

**Name:** Control \(\psi(p_{k+1}-1)-\psi(p_k)\) using prime-power counting and short-interval estimates.

**High-level idea:**

Taking logarithms, the inequality is
\[
\psi(p_{k+1}-1) - \psi(p_k) < \log p_k,
\]
where \(\psi(x)=\sum_{p^a\le x}\log p\) is Chebyshev’s second function and satisfies \(\log L(n)=\psi(n)\). 

But between \(p_k\) and \(p_{k+1}-1\) there are no primes, so the only contributions to \(\psi\) come from prime powers \(q^a\) with \(a\ge 2\). Thus,
\[
\psi(p_{k+1}-1)-\psi(p_k)=\sum_{\substack{q^a\in(p_k,p_{k+1})\\ a\ge 2}} \log q.
\]
So we need an upper bound on this “prime-power mass” inside prime gaps.

This approach aims not necessarily at a uniform-for-all-\(k\) proof, but at:
- showing the inequality holds for “almost all” \(k\) (density \(1\)),
- or proving it holds infinitely often,
- or proving it holds under hypotheses like Cramér, Elliott–Halberstam, etc.

**Detailed plan:**

1. **Convert to \(\psi\)-difference form** using \(\log L(n)=\psi(n)\). 

2. **Rewrite the difference as sum over prime powers** in the gap (as above).

3. **Bound contributions by exponent:**
   For each \(a\ge 2\),
   \[
   \sum_{\substack{q^a\in(p_k,p_{k+1})}} \log q
   \le 
   \theta(p_{k+1}^{1/a})-\theta(p_k^{1/a}),
   \]
   where \(\theta(x)=\sum_{p\le x}\log p\) is Chebyshev’s first function.

4. **Apply upper bounds for primes in short intervals** to \(\theta\) or \(\pi\) differences (e.g. Brun–Titchmarsh type bounds, or explicit PNT error terms) to show that for “typical” gaps the total is \(<\log p_k\).

5. **Use distribution results for prime gaps** (e.g. average gap \(\sim \log p_k\)) and for prime powers to show that large accumulations are rare, giving a density-1 statement.

6. **If allowed to assume conjectures:** under Cramér-type gap bounds, immediately force the square-root interval to be so short that the \(a=2\) contribution is \(0\) for large \(k\), and the rest is even smaller; then finish as in Approach 1.

**Required tools or theorems:**

- Chebyshev functions \(\psi,\theta\) and their relation to lcm.   
- Upper bounds for primes in short intervals (Brun–Titchmarsh inequality, PNT with explicit error terms, etc.).
- Results on distribution of prime gaps “on average” (Gallagher-type heuristics, or proven mean estimates).
- Possibly RH or stronger conjectures if one wants to push from “almost all” toward “all”.

**Main obstacles:**

- A uniform bound “for every prime gap” is far beyond present analytic methods: we can bound typical behavior, but eliminating all exceptional gaps (especially those containing prime squares) is extremely hard.
- Prime squares are the dominant obstruction: bounding \(\theta(\sqrt{p_{k+1}})-\theta(\sqrt{p_k})\) by \(\log p_k\) uniformly is essentially tantamount to strong constraints on primes in *very short* intervals around \(\sqrt{p_k}\).

**Expected difficulty:** High for partial/density results; Very High for a full “all \(k\)” statement.

**Estimated viability score:** 55/100 for meaningful partial theorems (“almost all \(k\)”, conditional statements); 10/100 for a complete unconditional proof.

**Notes on similarity to other approaches:** Related to Approach 1 via the \(\psi\)-reformulation, but distinct in focusing on global analytic bounds and “most \(k\)” statements rather than a clean combinatorial product bound.

### **Approach 3 (Index 3): Counterexample hunting / constructive disproof via gaps containing multiple prime squares (computational + heuristic)**

**Name:** Try to falsify by locating a prime gap containing two prime squares (or enough prime-power events).

**High-level idea:**

Because the ratio
\[
\frac{L(p_{k+1}-1)}{L(p_k)}
\]
is a product of primes \(q\) corresponding to prime powers \(q^a\) in the gap, a very concrete obstruction emerges:

- If there are **two primes** \(q_1<q_2\) such that
  \[
  p_k < q_1^2,\ q_2^2 < p_{k+1},
  \]
  then the ratio includes \(q_1q_2\), and since \(q_i>\sqrt{p_k}\), one gets \(q_1q_2>p_k\), giving an immediate counterexample.

This matches the Erdős–Graham remark that ruling out “many primes \(q\) with \(p_k<q^2<p_{k+1}\)” is a key difficulty. 

So a viable “attack” is to search computationally for such a configuration (or more generally check whether the product condition fails) among known large prime gaps.

**Detailed plan:**

1. **Implement the exact ratio test efficiently:**
   For each \(k\), compute the set
   \[
   S_k=\{q:\exists a\ge 2,\ p_k<q^a<p_{k+1}\},
   \]
   then check whether \(\prod_{q\in S_k}q < p_k\).

2. **Prioritize large prime gaps:**
   The inequality is easiest to violate when \(p_{k+1}-p_k\) is unusually large, because larger gaps are more likely to contain prime squares/cubes/etc.

3. **Fast detection of prime squares in a gap:**
   - Compute \(A=\lceil \sqrt{p_k+1}\rceil\), \(B=\lfloor\sqrt{p_{k+1}-1}\rfloor\).
   - Enumerate primes in \([A,B]\); if there are \(\ge 2\), you have a counterexample immediately.

4. **If no counterexamples via squares, extend to other powers:**
   check for primes \(q\) with \(q^3\) in the gap, etc. (Though squares are likely the main driver.)

5. **Heuristic guidance:**
   Use probabilistic models (Cramér model) to estimate how often a prime gap of size \(\gg \sqrt{x}\) occurs and how often it would contain \(\ge 2\) prime squares, guiding where to look.

6. **(Conditional constructive disproof idea):**
   Try to engineer long intervals of consecutive composites that contain two specific prime squares using CRT-type congruences, then invoke a prime \(k\)-tuple conjecture to argue existence of primes at endpoints. This would be conditional but could suggest whether counterexamples are expected.

**Required tools or theorems:**

- Computational prime generation / prime gap tables.
- Efficient primality testing and prime enumeration in intervals.
- Number-theoretic optimization for checking prime powers.

**Main obstacles:**

- If the statement is true (as Erdős–Graham suspect), no finite search will find a counterexample.
- Even if false, the first counterexample might occur at an astronomically large prime, well beyond practical computation.
- Constructively forcing both endpoints to be consecutive primes is hard; CRT constructions create composite runs but don’t directly yield the primes at the ends.

**Expected difficulty:** Medium (computational exploration); Very High (rigorous constructive disproof).

**Estimated viability score:** 45/100 (good for falsification attempts / evidence gathering; not guaranteed to resolve).

**Notes on similarity to other approaches:** Distinct (this is a falsification/computation-oriented strategy rather than a proof strategy).

  \]
  and estimate prime-power contributions in prime-free intervals.
- **Approach 3:** search for counterexamples by detecting two prime squares in the same prime gap (since that forces \(\prod q \ge p_k\)).

### Main gaps identified (solver + evaluator)
- The hard core is **prime squares inside prime gaps**: ruling out “many primes \(q\)” with \(p_k<q^2<p_{k+1}\) seems to require Legendre-type prime-gap strength.
- The “**small primes trouble**” is a second major obstacle: many distinct small primes might each have a power crossing in the same gap, making \(\prod_{q\in S_k}q\) large.
- No unconditional method is currently available to uniformly control prime powers inside *every* prime gap.

Externally, this problem is listed as **Erdős Problem #458** (Erdős–Graham, 1980, p.91) and is described as open; Erdős and Graham explicitly flag the same two obstacles (prime squares in gaps; small primes). ([erdosproblems.com](https://www.erdosproblems.com/458?utm_source=openai))

## 2. Key obstacles (as research questions)

1. **Prime squares in prime gaps**
   - *Research question:* What unconditional (or conditional) bounds are known for the number of primes \(q\) such that \(p_k<q^2<p_{k+1}\)?  
   - *Related difficulty:* Even proving “at most one such \(q\)” uniformly would follow from a Legendre-flavored prime gap bound \(p_{k+1}-p_k \ll \sqrt{p_k}\), which is far beyond current methods. ([erdosproblems.com](https://www.erdosproblems.com/458?utm_source=openai))

2. **Small primes / clustering of multiple prime powers**
   - *Research question:* Can one show that it is impossible (or extremely limited) for many distinct primes \(q\) (especially small \(q\)) to have a power \(q^{a(q)}\in(p_k,p_{k+1})\) simultaneously, because that would force unusually close “near-collisions” among distinct prime powers?
   - *Potential tool class:* linear forms in logarithms (Baker theory) / exponential Diophantine bounds.

3. **Known constructions of prime gaps containing prime powers**
   - *Research question:* What is known about the existence of prime gaps containing perfect powers or prime powers? Does existing large-gap technology allow multiple prime powers in one gap, or only one?

4. **Status check**
   - *Research question:* Is the inequality already proved, disproved, or computationally checked to a large range? (Externally it is listed open, but this should be rechecked against literature.)

### Key sources found and what they say

#### (A) Problem is explicitly posed as an open Erdős–Graham problem (#458)
- **Source:** ErdősProblems.com entry for #458, citing Erdős–Graham (1980), p.91.
- **Content:** The problem is stated exactly; Erdős and Graham are quoted (paraphrased) as believing it “almost certainly” true but beyond reach, citing:
  1) need to rule out many primes \(q\) with \(p_k<q^2<p_{k+1}\), and  
  2) “small primes also cause trouble.” ([erdosproblems.com](https://www.erdosproblems.com/458?utm_source=openai))
- **How this helps:** Confirms the internal evaluator’s assessment that the full “for all \(k\)” statement is plausibly **open** and that the two identified obstacles are the historically recognized ones.

#### (B) Prime gaps can be constructed to contain perfect powers (and even prime powers)
This is directly relevant to understanding how hard it is to control prime powers inside gaps.

1) **Ford–Heath-Brown–Konyagin (2014): “Large gaps between consecutive prime numbers containing perfect powers”**
- **Source:** arXiv:1411.6543 (HTML via ar5iv). ([ar5iv.org](https://ar5iv.org/pdf/1411.6543))  
- **Main statement (as given):** For any fixed \(k\), there are infinitely many perfect \(k\)-th powers inside very long gaps between consecutive primes; they formalize “prime avoiding” perfect powers and prove existence (Theorem 1 in their paper). ([ar5iv.org](https://ar5iv.org/pdf/1411.6543))
- **How it might apply:**  
  - It shows that one cannot hope to prove the Erdős–Graham inequality by claiming “prime gaps rarely contain perfect powers” in any absolute sense—large gaps **can** be arranged to contain perfect powers.
  - However, these gaps are still of polylogarithmic size (Rankin/Erdős–Rankin type), far smaller than \(\sqrt{x}\), so this does not immediately create the “two prime squares in one gap” obstruction.

2) **Maier–Rassias (2015): “Large gaps between consecutive prime numbers containing square-free numbers and perfect powers of prime numbers”**
- **Source:** arXiv:1504.01645 (HTML via ar5iv). ([ar5iv.org](https://ar5iv.org/pdf/1504.01645))  
- **Main statement (as given):** They prove, among other things, that for any \(k\) there are infinitely many **perfect \(k\)-th powers of prime numbers** which are “prime avoiding” with some constant (Theorem 1.2). ([ar5iv.org](https://ar5iv.org/pdf/1504.01645))
- **How it might apply:**
  - This strengthens (B1) in exactly the direction relevant to our lcm problem: it produces prime gaps containing numbers of the form \(p^k\), i.e. genuine **prime powers** (prime base). That is the precise “event type” that changes \(\mathrm{lcm}(1,\dots,n)\) across prime-free stretches.
  - It suggests that prime squares \(q^2\) *do* occur inside some prime gaps (infinitely often), so any attempted proof must tolerate at least occasional square events and must instead control the *product* of all such base primes.

#### (C) Known large-gap technology (context on feasible gap sizes)
- **Source:** Maynard (2014), “Large gaps between primes” (arXiv:1408.5110). ([arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/1408.5110?utm_source=openai))  
- **Content:** For any fixed \(t\), there are consecutive primes \(p<p'\le x\) with
  \[
  p'-p \ge t(1+o(1))\frac{(\log x)(\log\log x)(\log\log\log\log x)}{(\log\log\log x)^2}.
  \]
  ([arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/1408.5110?utm_source=openai))
- **How it might apply:**
  - Reinforces that prime gaps can be much larger than \(\log x\), but still \(o(x^\varepsilon)\) for every \(\varepsilon>0\).
  - In particular, these known lower bounds are vastly smaller than \(\sqrt{x}\), supporting the heuristic that **two squares** in a single prime gap should be extraordinarily unlikely—though this is far from a proof.

#### (D) Linear forms in logarithms (Baker theory) as a potential tool for “small primes trouble”
This line looks promising for addressing the evaluator’s “small primes” gap, because “many small prime power crossings in one gap” forces unusually close prime powers.

- **Source 1 (primary):** A. Baker, “Linear forms in the logarithms of algebraic numbers” (Mathematika 1966). The tool search surfaced a direct PDF link. ([cambridge.org](https://www.cambridge.org/core/services/aop-cambridge-core/content/view/55674A9E93007ADD34DCEDC12EA4FB4D/S0025579300003971a.pdf/linear_forms_in_the_logarithms_of_algebraic_numbers.pdf?utm_source=openai))  
- **Source 2 (background monograph):** Shorey & Tijdeman, *Exponential Diophantine Equations* (Cambridge Tracts in Mathematics 87; print 1986). ([cambridge.org](https://www.cambridge.org/core/books/exponential-diophantine-equations/8A5FC7D12D90E0CFFDF272648A01E768?utm_source=openai))  
- **Source 3 (summary statement):** Encyclopedia of Mathematics entry “Linear form in logarithms,” describing explicit lower bounds of Baker-type. ([encyclopediaofmath.org](https://encyclopediaofmath.org/wiki/Linear_form_in_logarithms?utm_source=openai))  

**What these results typically give (high-level):**
- If \(a,b\ge 2\) are fixed integers and \(m,n\) are large, then a small difference
  \[
  |a^m-b^n| \ \text{“very small”}
  \]
  implies that the linear form
  \[
  |m\log a - n\log b|
  \]
  is extremely small. Baker’s theory provides **explicit lower bounds** on nonzero linear forms in logarithms of algebraic numbers, hence forbids such extreme closeness except possibly for boundedly many exceptional cases. ([cambridge.org](https://www.cambridge.org/core/services/aop-cambridge-core/content/view/55674A9E93007ADD34DCEDC12EA4FB4D/S0025579300003971a.pdf/linear_forms_in_the_logarithms_of_algebraic_numbers.pdf?utm_source=openai))

**How this might apply to the lcm inequality:**
- If the prime gap \((p_k,p_{k+1})\) contains powers of two distinct small primes \(a\) and \(b\), say \(a^{m}\) and \(b^{n}\), then these two prime powers lie within distance \(p_{k+1}-p_k\) of each other.  
- For gap sizes in regimes like \(p_{k+1}-p_k \ll p_k^\alpha\) with \(\alpha<1\), Baker-type lower bounds often imply that two distinct fixed-base powers cannot be that close once the scale is large enough (heuristically: \(|a^m-b^n|\) is typically \(\gg a^m/(\log a^m)^C\), which eventually dominates \(p_k^\alpha\)). Making this rigorous would require choosing an explicit Baker–Wüstholz/Baker bound and tracing constants.

**Caveat:** This does not resolve the prime-square obstruction because the base \(q\) in \(q^2\) is not fixed; Baker theory is most effective when the bases are fixed (e.g. \(2^m\) vs \(3^n\)). So this is specifically a tool candidate for the “small primes trouble” subproblem.

## 4. Impact on current solution method

1. **Supports the “problem is genuinely hard / likely open” conclusion.**  
   The ErdősProblems entry explicitly classifies #458 as open and repeats essentially the same obstacles the evaluator identified. ([erdosproblems.com](https://www.erdosproblems.com/458?utm_source=openai))

2. **Shows that prime powers inside prime gaps are not an avoidable phenomenon.**  
   Ford–Heath-Brown–Konyagin and Maier–Rassias prove that long prime gaps can be constructed to contain perfect powers, and even **prime powers of primes**. ([ar5iv.org](https://ar5iv.org/pdf/1411.6543))  
   This means any proof must *bound the multiplicative contribution* of such events rather than attempt to forbid them altogether.

3. **Suggests a concrete refinement for the “small primes” gap: invoke Baker/logarithmic forms.**  
   The evaluator noted that the “small primes trouble” was a placeholder in Approach 1. Baker theory provides an established toolkit to show distinct fixed-base prime powers cannot lie too close, via explicit lower bounds for linear forms in logarithms. ([cambridge.org](https://www.cambridge.org/core/services/aop-cambridge-core/content/view/55674A9E93007ADD34DCEDC12EA4FB4D/S0025579300003971a.pdf/linear_forms_in_the_logarithms_of_algebraic_numbers.pdf?utm_source=openai))  
   This could be turned into a rigorous lemma of the form:  
   *for sufficiently large \(x\), an interval \([x,x+x^\alpha]\) cannot contain both a power of 2 and a power of 3*, etc. That would drastically constrain how many *distinct small primes* can simultaneously contribute to \(S_k\).

4. **Does not remove the fundamental square obstruction.**  
   The literature found does not give any unconditional way to uniformly bound primes \(q\) with \(p_k<q^2<p_{k+1}\). Erdős–Graham themselves explicitly connect this to Legendre-type difficulty. ([erdosproblems.com](https://www.erdosproblems.com/458?utm_source=openai))

## 5. Suggested new or refined approaches

### Approach A: “Baker-theory control of the small primes”
**Core idea:**  
Turn “small primes cause trouble” into a precise exponential-Diophantine lemma: if a prime gap contains prime powers of two (or many) fixed small bases, then two distinct perfect powers are unusually close, contradicting explicit lower bounds from linear forms in logarithms.

**External tools to leverage:**
- Baker’s 1966 theorem framework for linear forms in logarithms. ([cambridge.org](https://www.cambridge.org/core/services/aop-cambridge-core/content/view/55674A9E93007ADD34DCEDC12EA4FB4D/S0025579300003971a.pdf/linear_forms_in_the_logarithms_of_algebraic_numbers.pdf?utm_source=openai))  
- Shorey–Tijdeman’s monograph for how to turn such bounds into explicit inequalities on \(|a^m-b^n|\). ([cambridge.org](https://www.cambridge.org/core/books/exponential-diophantine-equations/8A5FC7D12D90E0CFFDF272648A01E768?utm_source=openai))  
- Encyclopedia of Mathematics summary bounds for linear forms in logs. ([encyclopediaofmath.org](https://encyclopediaofmath.org/wiki/Linear_form_in_logarithms?utm_source=openai))  

**What it would aim to prove internally:**
- For each pair of small primes \(a<b\) (say \(2,3\); \(2,5\); \(3,5\); …), there exists \(X(a,b)\) such that for all \(x>X(a,b)\), the interval \((x, x+x^\alpha)\) (for some \(\alpha<1\) that you can plug in from a prime-gap upper bound you are willing to assume) contains **at most one** of \(\{a^m : m\ge 2\}\cup\{b^n : n\ge 2\}\).
- Conclude: beyond a finite range, \(S_k\) cannot contain “too many” small primes simultaneously.

**Main remaining hurdle:**  
You still need either (i) some upper bound on the prime gap \(p_{k+1}-p_k\) to choose \(\alpha\), or (ii) an argument that even if the gap is large, accumulating many small-prime factors is still insufficient unless squares appear (shifting burden back to the square obstruction).

---

### Approach B: “Conditional closure under strong prime-gap hypotheses + finite verification”
**Core idea:**  
Under a Cramér/Firoozbakht-strength gap upper bound \(p_{k+1}-p_k \ll (\log p_k)^2\), the root intervals \(p_k^{1/a} \to p_{k+1}^{1/a}\) become so short that eventually there are **no** (or at most one) prime power crossings at all. Then only finitely many \(k\) remain.

**External context:**
- Maynard/Ford–Green–Tao etc show large gaps can be polylogarithmic but do not contradict Cramér-type upper bounds. ([arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/1408.5110?utm_source=openai))  

**What it would aim to produce:**
- A clean conditional theorem: “Assuming hypothesis \(H\) on prime gaps, the inequality holds for all sufficiently large \(k\).”
- Then computationally check remaining \(k\le k_0(H)\).

**Main remaining hurdle:**  
This doesn’t address the unconditional “all \(k\)” goal; it produces a conditional result.

---

### Approach C: “Try for a density-1 (almost all \(k\)) result”
**Core idea:**  
Instead of bounding every gap, show that the set of \(k\) for which \(\prod_{q\in S_k}q \ge p_k\) has density 0, by bounding the frequency of prime-power crossings in typical prime gaps.

**External context:**
- Known work on prime gaps discusses distributional behavior (e.g. Pintz’s work on normalized gaps / limit points), but translating that into uniform control of prime powers is nontrivial. ([arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/1407.2213?utm_source=openai))  

**Main remaining hurdle:**  
You need a robust probabilistic/analytic framework to count “prime powers falling into prime-free intervals,” and it’s not clear existing theorems are strong enough without major new input.
